# Pedagogy

Riff doesn't follow a single school of thought. It combines six complementary approaches, each covering something the others don't.

---

## Framework

**Project-Based Learning (PBL)**
The learning plan is organized around real songs as projects. You don't practice abstract skills — you learn exactly what you need to play something with a name, something that moves you. The song is always the destination.

**Mastery-Based Learning**
Progress is not measured by time spent but by demonstrated mastery. You move forward when you've shown you're ready — not before. A sustained accuracy threshold determines when a skill is solid enough to build on.

**Suzuki Method**
Listen first, play after. Always. You can't reproduce well what you haven't internalized aurally. Before you play anything in Riff, you hear it. This is non-negotiable.

**Deliberate Practice** *(Ericsson)*
The goal is not to practice a lot but to practice the specific weak point with immediate feedback. Every session targets something concrete. Nothing is practiced for its own sake.

**Spaced Repetition** *(Ebbinghaus)*
What you've learned is reintroduced just before you forget it. Riff tracks this invisibly — you never manage it, you never see it. It just works.

**Self-Determination Theory** *(Deci & Ryan)*
Three forces drive intrinsic motivation: autonomy (you choose the songs), competence (difficulty adapts so you're always in the learning zone), and purpose (the goal is always visible, never abstract). Riff is designed around all three.

---

## Learning structure

A learning plan in Riff is organized around two types of sessions.

**Deliberate practice sessions** focus on the chords, transitions, and techniques needed for the transfer task ahead. Each session ends by playing a fragment of the target song — the project always in sight, never abstract.

**Transfer tasks** are the songs themselves. You play the full song or an agreed fragment in real context. When you clear the mastery threshold, you unlock the next level of the skill graph. The same song can appear as a transfer task multiple times with increasing demands — first the intro, then the solo, then full speed.

Clearing a transfer task unlocks the next branch of the skill graph.

---

## The turn principle

Riff never plays and listens at the same time. Every exercise has two sequential and exclusive phases: Riff plays, then you play. This follows directly from the Suzuki principle — the ear must know where it's going before the hands try to get there.
